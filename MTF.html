    • Magnetized Target Fusion (MTF)

    Combining MCF and ICF results in magnetized target fusion. A fusion fuel is confined at a lower density by magnetic fields and heated to become plasma. Fusion reactions are initiated by rapidly squeezing this fuel target to greatly raise the density and temperature. The result is at a far lower density than those of ICF and theoretically it will combine longer confinement times and better heat retention than the previous two methods.

    One example of this is General Fusion's MTF reactor[1] which is comprised of 3 main components. Firstly, the plasma injector turns hydrogen fuel into hot plasma confining it in a magnetic field. Next, there is a chamber of spinning liquid metal which is used to compress the plasma as well as capturing the energy produced by the fusion reactions. A compression system is used to pump the liquid metal and form a vortex. Finally, a group of pistons are used in order to collapse the vortex, compressing the plasma further in the process. These pistons have to strike as close as possible to the same time as each other to create a symmetrical shockwave to interact with the fuel. To obtain energy from this the liquid metal wall is pumped through a heat exchanger to turn a steam turbine similar to traditional power plants.

    Field-reversed Configuration (FRC)[3]

    FRC is used for magnetized target fusion. It is a device which confines the plasma on closed magnetic field lines without a central penetration so that it is a self-stable torus shape. The plasma is confined in it's own magnetic field by inducing a toroidal electric current in a plasma cylinder. This method is similar to spheromaks except that they have an internal toroidal field. (Spheromaks are essentially a spherical tokamak without a central conductor or magnet.) Field-reversed configuration is less prone to instabilities in theory but struggles to make a sufficiently dense and stable plasma in practice.

    Issues with MTF

    Magnetised target fusion has the benefit of combining the advantages of internal and magnetic confinement such that fusion becomes more stable and easier to attain them individually. On the other hand, the amount of secondary or auxiliary systems needed to sustain the reaction, being the superconducting magnetic field and laser array needed to fuse the D-T mixture, increases. Consequently, the net energy gain needed for commercialisation becomes more difficult. It would require the reactor to run for long periods, something which is still being worked on in a research setting. All the while, the issues of these techniques still need to be addressed to improve efficiency, which is discussed further in the magnetic confinement page and internal confinement page.
