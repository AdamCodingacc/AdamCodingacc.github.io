    • Inertial Confinement Fusion (ICF)

    This type of nuclear fusion attempts to initiate a fusion reaction by heating up and compressing a fuel target (in the form of a pellet). These pellets will be compressed to incredibly high densities and temperatures so that fusion occurs for a short period of time (in the region of nanoseconds) until they blow apart. To achieve this compression high intensity lasers or beams of charged particles (a driver) are aimed at the outer layer of the target. The heated outer layer will explode, producing a reaction force which acts on the remaining fuel. This compresses it via shockwaves which are sufficiently powerful to compress and heat the fuel enough for fusion. This method of energy delivery can be direct or indirect depending on the setup and in its final stages can compress the pellet in the region of 1000 to 10,000 times the density of a typical solid.

    Direct delivery is when the laser is shone directly onto the surface of the fuel pellet. Indirect drive is when the laser is shone onto another object called a hohlraum (a gold cavity) which contains the pellet. It then vaporises bathing the fuel in X-rays, compressing it and starting fusion. This method is used at NIF (National Ignition Facility), the largest example of an ICF currently operational, as it prevents hydrodynamic instability by uniform exposure.

    The fuel target generally consists of a mixture of deuterium and tritium. Pellets of fuel are normally around 10 mg and 1 to 10 mm in diameter. To be efficient the time the pellet is burning must be less than the time for disassembly. To have this condition met, compressed pellet mass density x radius must be greater than 3 g/cm2. This helps because a large mass density hastens the burning of the pellet and a larger radius decreases the disassembly time. In order to be worthwhile the ratio of energy produced to driver energy recquired (pellet gain) has to be over 100.

    Issues with ICF

    First Light Fusion

    First light fusion have tried to use a "pistol shrimp mechanism." This draws back the hammer part and then snaps to the opposite claw creating a rapid pressure change which causes vapor-filled voids in water. These are called cavitation bubbles which collapse to produce shock waves. Vapor is compressed so forcefully that it causes plasma to form – earth’s only natural example of ICF. Plasma formed in this way reaches temperatures of around 4,800 °C. First light fusion are trying to use this design for a reactor to be the shockwave in ICF. It is intended to trigger the fusion reaction by creation of inertially confined plasma.
