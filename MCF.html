    • Magnetic Confinement Fusion (MCF)

    MCF is the other most common method of fusion and the most highly developed currently. Tokamaks are the main example in current use such as the one being built at ITER and the one at JET. MCF uses magnetic fields to contain the fuel in the form of a hot plasma. It uses the conductivity of plasma to control it through the way it interacts with external magnetic fields. Pressure from magnetism is used to counteract that which originates from the plasma to try and hold the fuel with as little turbulence and leaking effects as possible. There are two main types of MCF reactor: Magnetic mirror devices and Toroidal machines.

    Magnetic Mirror Devices

    Mirror devices generate non-planar fields generated by solenoids in which the strength of the field is raised at either end. As charged particles approach either end of the magnetic field they are reflected to keep them contained. Unfortunately, particles which are moving too fast along the field cannot be stopped by the mirror and escape leading to large leaking effects. Sections of plasma can be added at either end past the 'mirror' to reduce the leaking via the electrostatic potential barrier they produce. These are called end plugs, providing electrostatic plugging. This set up is called a "tandem mirror" design but it is not perfect. In fact this design did not prove fruitful and little work has been carried out in this field since the 1970s.

    Toroidal Machines

    Currently, the most popular method are Toroidal machines, of which Tokamaks are the most widespread example. These machines use a variety of set ups to produce a toroidal magnetic field in order to contain the plasma fuel.

    Tokamak Reactors

    The term tokamak comes from a Russian acronym standing for "toroidal chamber with magnetic coils" as this is what comprises the reactor (a toroid being a roughly doughnut shaped object.) A Tokamak reactor produces energy through fusion which is absorbed into the walls and used to produce steam. This steam turns large turbines similar to a traditional power plant (e.g. coal-fired power station.)

    The heart of the reactor is a doughnut shaped vacuum chamber. Inside this ring the environment is very hot and under extremely high pressure. Under these conditions hydrogen gas becomes a plasma (hot and electrically charged gas. This allows light elements to fuse in a star.) Charged plasma particles can be altered by large magnetic coils to keep the hot plasma from touching the vessel walls.

    To start with air and other impurites are evacuated from the chamber. Magnetic systems which will help with confining and keeping the plasma under control are charged up. Gaseous fuel is introduced when the magnets are charged up. When the fuel is introduced a very large electrical current is run through the vessel causing the hydrogen gas to break down electrically. The nuclei lose electrons through ionization to become plasma in the reactor. When this plasma becomes energized the individual particles collide and heat up.

    External support methods help heat the plasma to above 150 million Â°C as this is the range in which fusion becomes possible. It happens at these temperatures because the plasma can now overcome the natural electromagnetic repulsion between particles. This allows the particles to fuse on collisions to release energy.

    Stellarator

    Stellarators are an example of MCF and more specifically a toroidal machine. Unlike Tokamaks, a stellarator is axially asymmetric and should eliminate most of the particle drift which is an issue in tokamaks. It does this by using external magnetic coils only creating a negligible plasma current. It is comprised of spiralling ribbon shapes which produce high-density plasma which is symmetric and more stable than tokamaks allowing for a longer running duration. Geometry makes these difficult to build and highly sensitive. The original shape was two half Torus shapes so the drift up on the inside edge of the tube and down on the outside edge will cancel out. There are issues with this however as it can cause collisions hence the revised shape.

    Issues with MCF

    The main issue of the magnetic confinement is the containment of the plasma using superconducting magnets, hence magnetic confinement. The charged particles in the plasma beam experience a Lorentz force as they accelerate in a circular direction around the reactor. This force causes them to remain in the centre of the vessel, balanced between the inward force of the magnetic field and pressure from the plasma pushing back.[britannica]

    The issue with this is that if the reaction rate is not uniform throughout the plasma it can lead to pressure fluctuations and therefore an unstable plasma. This can cause quenching of the reaction if it touches the vessel walls of the reactor, allowing heat to escape and cause fusion to stop. For example, at the JET fusion reactor, which has a plasma density 1 million times less dense than air, it means any small change in density of the plasma could become problematic[CCFE]. A solution is to use more individual magnets around the reactor and monitor for fluctuations of the plasma and calibrate the B field. This comes at an energy cost as superconducting magnets need to have temperatures near absolute zero, which becomes costly if the reactor is not running continuously.
