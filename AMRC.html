    Introduction to research done at the AMRC

    AMRC is the Advanced Manufacturing Research Centre run by the University of Sheffield. It performs general research as well as collaborating with specific companies to try and improve the manufacturing abilities of the world. It is the site the UKAEA has chosen to invest in a new facility in order to research nuclear related projects and manufacturing techniques.[1]

    In the new UKAEA facility, research will primarily consist of finding the right materials for nuclear fusion, joining techniques for materials, additive manufacturing (AM), advanced numerical simulation to predict component performance and improving codes and standards to make nuclear fusion commercially viable.[2]

    The NAMRC is working together with the UKAEA on the feasibility of modular construction techniques. Together, they are studying how the modularisation techniques used for current modular reactors could be applied to STEP. The research aims to reduce the risk in the manufacturing and construction stages of the reactor.

    Finding the right materials for a commercial reactor is a difficult task. Conditions in the reactor walls reach temperatures of up to 2000 degrees C. Refractory metals are the most suitable choice of materials, with melting points of 2000 degrees but there are many other requirements a suitable material must meet to be safely implemented in a reactor. The advanced analysis and assessment of material requires extremely sensitive equipment like graphene sensors. The new facility in the Advanced Manufacturing Park will carry out research testing various materials under the conditions in a nuclear reactor. Currently the new facility has been finished being built and the UKAEA is currently fitting it out, an official opening will be held when it is safe to do so.

    Additive Manufacturing

    Additive manufacturing (AM), also known as 3D printing, will be heavily researched and used as a method of designing complex components needed for nuclear fusion[3]. The term for additive manufacturing comes from construction of materials, layer by layer. Some aircraft and spacecraft already make use of additive manufacturing for their components, whilst nuclear research mostly depends on materials developed decades ago. The Rotherham facility will be researching this technique for nuclear fusion. AM is still relatively new and will be key in designing components. This could be done in conjunction with the department of mechanical engineering and more specifically the Centre for advanced additive manufacturing (AdAM)[4].

    AM occurs by using a thin layer of powder metals (~20-30 microns) and dropping them onto a substrate. A laser or an electron beam is then used to melt the powder into fully formed components. Metal additive manufacturing is a better alternative to traditional manufacturing techniques as using this process saves time and money. Furthermore, the components are designed in a software of your choice, layer by layer, giving engineers a better understanding of the materials and components being made and more customisability to make bespoke materials. This is a quick process, where engineers can have the component produced in a matter of hours. This significantly reduces the time requirements in the whole process of designing and testing materials. In turn this is better for making alterations to parts produced as more traditional methods will be more costly to make changes to the design. This is because the tools (casts) are made specifically for that component.

    With AM, you can optimize the structure more efficiently. The difference in cost between complex and simple components is not overly significant. This has had practical applications in aviation by reducing the mass of components in the aircraft, hence lowering the amount of fuel needed to carry the craft. Nuclear fusion can benefit from the ability to design more complex components easily as each reactor is a bespoke facility and therefore does not greatly benefit from traditional mass manufacturing.

    At the advanced manufacturing research centre (AMRC), a 3D woven composite component is currently being developed alongside the UKAEA[5]. They set out to design a component that could withstand 600 degrees celsius and using additive manufacturing were able to produce a complex component that met the design specifications using silicon carbide. When the new facility is running, this is the research that will be carried out. Standard manufacturing routes are struggling to meet the requirements to achieve commercial fusion thus newer manufacturing routes have to be researched to achieve this.

    Powder Production

    For additive manufacturing, we need to use powder metals. The University of Sheffield department of materials science and engineering is a founding part of the Henry Royce Institute where research involves that into powder production[6]. They research several methods, such as gas atomisation, it produces spherical powders from titanium, iron etc. This method is useful for powder production as the method is inert and can produce powders from titanium alloys and refractory metals, which are metals with high temperatures (>2000 degrees celsius) amongst other useful properties.

    Plasma Spheroidisation is also researched which uses high energy plasma to produce highly spherical and dense powders. It provides consistent results and these powders have better flowability inside an AM machine, reducing production time.

    Attrition milling is also researched at the University of Sheffield, a more general technique for powder production. It is more appropriate for mid-range sized particles such as carbon steel, stainless steel, chrome and tungsten. At the UKAEA facility they could work with the University of Sheffield to find appropriate methods for powder production for the additive manufacturing needs for nuclear fusion.

    Joining Techniques

    Another avenue of research at the centre, will be joining techniques for materials. Particular interest is in advanced power beam welding techniques, such as electron beam welding and laser welding. This is in comparison to manual arc welding, which is commonly used as in most design nuclear design codes. The UKAEA hopes to explore techniques like this to innovate manufacturing technologies in the facility being constructed at the AMRC.

    Electron Beam Welding

    Electron beam welding for example is much more efficient than manual arc welding. An electron beam is emitted from a cathode in a vacuum. These electrons are accelerated via a voltage and focused using an electromagnetic coil. These electrons transfer heat when they collide with the surface of the material in question. Manual arc welding uses arc discharge to weld materials. A voltage is applied to 2 electrodes, this voltage is increased until the dielectric breakdown of air between the electrodes, this emits heat and light which is used to weld the material. With electron beam welding, the beam is more intense than arc welding. It has approximately a 1000 times greater energy density with the heat applied to the surrounding areas being low, hence reducing distortion to the material. The output of the electron can be adjusted, allowing electron beam welding to be useful for a large selection of materials.

    Although electron beam welding is easier to control and more efficient, it requires a vacuum. There has been interest in local vacuum chambers so as to not vacuum the whole assembly of components but rather sections of it. More recently, there have been some electron beam welding machines capable of welding in a low vacuum. Research at the AMRC could look into this to help improve the feasibility of a commercial nuclear fusion reactor by reducing production costs and reducing distortion to create more precisely shaped components. This will have a secondary effect of increasing the safety of reactors too.

    Bonding Techniques

    Materials which are dissimilar to each other will have to be joined and for this solid state bonding techniques will be used and researched at the centre. Such techniques as diffusion bonding, ultrasonic welding, magnetic pulse welding and hot isostatic pressing can be looked into. Diffusion bonding relies on high temperatures and pressures to get the atoms of 2 layers to disperse into each other to weld them. Ultrasonic welding is where high frequency acoustic frequencies are used to melt the materials at the points of contact, no adhesive or screws are required. It is commonly used for the joining of thermoplastics. Magnetic pulse welding entails using EM pulses to create high density magnetic fields in the region to induce a pressure on the materials to join them. Hot isostatic materials will use high temperature and isostatic gas pressure to go beyond the yield strength of the materials to weld them. Using and researching these methods will enable us to fuse similar and dissimilar materials together hence allowing cheaper, quicker production of complex components which will help achieve nuclear fusion as a commercial reality.

    Design Codes

    To take nuclear fusion from research to commercial will require the aid of suitably designed codes and standards. Some of the current design standards were written decades ago. For example, arc welding is required by some codes for the design of nuclear reactors but, as spoken about previously, electron beam and laser welding are much more efficient that arc welding. Due to the current design specifications in use this mean arc welding will be used despite the clear benefits of the alternate methods. At the new UKAEA facility in the AMRC design codes and standards will be reviewed to cut down on cost of components, in order to produce more efficient, complex and safe components. There have been prior reviews to the approach of design specifications, such as by the Culham centre for fusion energy (CCFE) which suggests design time could be reduced by 4 orders of magnitude.[7] The ITER program uses the AFCEN codes, but to make nuclear fusion a commercial reality, many new design codes and specifications will need to be developed for these projects to take advantage of new technology and manufacturing capabilities.
