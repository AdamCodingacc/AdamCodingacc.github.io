Current Fusion Methods

Intro to fusion physics

Nuclear fusion reactions occur to combine light atomic nuclei, releasing energy in the process. To overcome the natural electrostatic repulsion between the reaction nuclei they have to reach extremely high temperatures (millions of degrees) to form a plasma. There are multiple methods for creating and forming this plasma currently being tested.

Inertial Confinement Fusion (ICF)

This type of nuclear fusion attempts to initiate a fusion reaction by heating up and compressing a fuel target (in the form of a pellet). These pellets will be compressed to incredibly high densities and temperatures so that fusion occurs for a short period of time (in the region of nanoseconds) until they blow apart. To achieve this compression high intensity lasers or beams of charged particles (a driver) are aimed at the outer layer of the target. The heated outer layer will explode, producing a reaction force which acts on the remaining fuel. This compresses it via shockwaves which are sufficiently powerful to compress and heat the fuel enough for fusion. This method of energy delivery can be direct or indirect depending on the setup and in its final stages can compress the pellet in the region of 1000 to 10,000 times the density of a typical solid.

Direct delivery is when the laser is shone directly onto the surface of the fuel pellet. Indirect drive is when the laser is shone onto another object called a hohlraum (a gold cavity) which contains the pellet. It then vaporises bathing the fuel in X-rays, compressing it and starting fusion. This method is used at NIF (National Ignition Facility), the largest example of an ICF currently operational, as it prevents hydrodynamic instability by uniform exposure.

The fuel target generally consists of a mixture of deuterium and tritium. Pellets of fuel are normally around 10 mg and 1 to 10 mm in diameter. To be efficient the time the pellet is burning must be less than the time for disassembly. To have this condition met, compressed pellet mass density x radius must be greater than 3 g/cm2. This helps because a large mass density hastens the burning of the pellet and a larger radius decreases the disassembly time. In order to be worthwhile the ratio of energy produced to driver energy recquired (pellet gain) has to be over 100.

Issues with ICF

First Light Fusion

First light fusion have tried to use a "pistol shrimp mechanism." This draws back the hammer part and then snaps to the opposite claw creating a rapid pressure change which causes vapor-filled voids in water. These are called cavitation bubbles which collapse to produce shock waves. Vapor is compressed so forcefully that it causes plasma to form – earth’s only natural example of ICF. Plasma formed in this way reaches temperatures of around 4,800 °C. First light fusion are trying to use this design for a reactor to be the shockwave in ICF. It is intended to trigger the fusion reaction by creation of inertially confined plasma.

Magnetic Confinement Fusion (MCF)

MCF is the other most common method of fusion and the most highly developed currently. Tokamaks are the main example in current use such as the one being built at ITER and the one at JET. MCF uses magnetic fields to contain the fuel in the form of a hot plasma. It uses the conductivity of plasma to control it through the way it interacts with external magnetic fields. Pressure from magnetism is used to counteract that which originates from the plasma to try and hold the fuel with as little turbulence and leaking effects as possible.

There are two main types of MCF reactor: Magnetic mirror devices and Toroidal machines. Mirror devices generate non-planar fields generated by solenoids in which the strength of the field is raised at either end. As charged particles approach either end of the magnetic field they are reflected to keep them contained. Unfortunately, particles which are moving too fast along the field cannot be stopped by the mirror and escape leading to large leaking effects. Sections of plasma can be added at either end past the 'mirror' to reduce the leaking via the electrostatic potential barrier they produce. These are called end plugs, providing electrostatic plugging. This set up is called a "tandem mirror" design but it is not perfect. In fact this design did not prove fruitful and little work has been carried out in this field since the 1970s.

Currently, the most popular method are Toroidal machines, of which Tokamaks are the most widespread example.

Stellarator

Stellarators are an example of MCF and more specifically a toroidal machine. Unlike Tokamaks a stellarator is axially asymmetric and should eliminate most of the particle drift which is an issue in tokamaks. It is comprised of spiralling ribbon shapes which produce high-density plasma which is symmetric and more stable than tokamaks allowing for a longer running duration. Geometry makes these difficult to build and highly sensitive. The original shape was two half Torus shapes so the drift up on the inside edge of the tube and down on the outside edge will cancel out. There are issues with this however as it can cause collisions hence the revised shape.

Magnetized Target Fusion (MTF)

Combining MCF and ICF results in magnetized target fusion. A fusion fuel is confined at a lower density by magnetic fields and heated to become plasma. Fusion reactions are initiated by rapidly squeezing this fuel target to greatly raise the density and temperature. The result is at a far lower density than those of ICF and theoretically it will combine longer confinement times and better heat retention than the previous two methods.

One example of this is General Fusion's MTF reactor which is comprised of 3 main components. Firstly, the plasma injector turns hydrogen fuel into hot plasma confining it in a magnetic field. Next, there is a chamber of spinning liquid metal which is used to compress the plasma as well as capturing the energy produced by the fusion reactions. A compression system is used to pump the liquid metal and form a vortex. Finally, a group of pistons are used in order to collapse the vortex, compressing the plasma further in the process. These pistons have to strike as close as possible to the same time as each other to create a symmetrical shockwave to interact with the fuel. To obtain energy from this the liquid metal wall is pumped through a heat exchanger to turn a steam turbine similar to traditional power plants.

Problems with MTF

One of the key issues is keeping plasma stable so it keeps its heat and lasts long enough to be able to be compressed.

Field-reversed Configuration (FRC)

FRC is used for magnetized target fusion. It is a device which confines the plasma on closed magnetic field lines without a central penetration so that it is a self-stable torus shape. The plasma is confined in it's own magnetic field by inducing a toroidal electric current in a plasma cylinder. This method is similar to spheromaks except that they have an internal toroidal field. (Spheromaks are essentially a spherical tokamak without a central conductor or magnet.) Field-reversed configuration is less prone to instabilities in theory but sturggles to make a sufficiently dense and stable plasma in practice.
