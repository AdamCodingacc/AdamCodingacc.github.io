    Introduction to the UKAEA

    The UKAEA (United Kingdom Atomic Energy authority) are a branch of the government dedicated to delivering nuclear fusion as a sustainable energy source. They aim to bring the UK to the front line of research and become world leaders in nuclear fusions development, creating jobs, sustainable energy and economic growth in the process. To do this they participate in many research projects, hosting most of them themselves. More recently this has included the £22 million fusion research facility under construction at the advanced manufacturing park in Rotherham. [1]

    Investments from the UKAEA

    On 27th June 2018 HM Government announced the ‘Nuclear sector deal’ which would aim to boost research and development in nuclear technologies and STEM in the UK. This deal included plans to raise research & development funding to 2.4% of GDP by 2027 and invest £725m into local innovation projects[2]. One of these projects is the £22m UKAEA fusion technology research facility currently under construction at the Advanced Manufacturing park (AMP) in Rotherham. The facility is being constructed by JF Vinnegan; a locally based design and construction firm and will be managed and funded by the UK Atomic Energy Authority. In addition to the promised funding packages provided by the Nuclear Sector Deal the project has also received guarantee of a £2m investment from Sheffield city’s local growth fund which aims to boost local business and allow for prosperous growth in the Sheffield city region. This investment is critical to allow the facility to complete its aim to develop and test joining technologies for fusion materials and components which can be tested under fusion conditions. This will, in turn, feed information and best practice to ITER, the international nuclear fusion test facility, to develop sustainable and mass-produced energy from nuclear fusion. Currently, local construction firms have completed the 25,000 sq ft exterior shell of the facility which is ready to be handed over to the UKAEA who are to begin to collate researchers and maintenance staff with the aim of commencing operation in 2021. The facility is strategically placed in the advanced manufacturing park where it can work alongside the University of Sheffield and the Nuclear Advanced Manufacturing Research Centre (NAMRC) as well as cooperating with the neighbouring Rolls Royce and Boeing facilites.

    The investment and construction of this facility comes as the government pushes to rapidly develop carbon-neutral forms of energy production and efficient ways of using nuclear sources to generate power. This is not the only investment in research into this area provided by the UKAEA. In 2017 the government announced an £86m investment to boost the UKAEA’s funding of a new fusion research programme at the Culham science centre which opened its doors this year. Another example of attention and large investment into the fusion sector is the government’s £220m investment in the UKAEA’s Spherical Tokamak for energy production (STEP) programme. These sudden investments came after a report was filed outlining the outcome and success of investment in nuclear fusion over the last decade. Clearly, the funding is available and increasing for not only energy production but in research and development as well.

    In a press release published on 24th November 2020, the government has announced that for every £1 invested into developing fusion technology in the UK, £4 is returned to boost the British economy. Using this claim, it can be predicted that the government's £346.7m investment in nuclear fusion over the past decade will return £1.4bn to the economy. The fact that this funding is returning so prosperously to the economy shows that investing in fusion research is, in fact, beneficial to the taxpayer and an efficient sector. This will surely result in a future of increased funding being directed towards nuclear fusion, causing an acceleration of attention and progress in developing efficient nuclear power. As a result of this, the University of Sheffield should endeavour to involve itself in the progression of nuclear fusion as it begins to gain more traction. This would start by sharing expertise and involving itself in the UKAEA facility at the AMP. Furthermore, the Government has committed to developing ‘Fusion-adjacent’ technologies such as robotics, computing and materials to work alongside direct fusion funding. This opens even more doors for the involvement of institutions like the University of Sheffield to enhance its current ongoing research in those areas as well as increasing work in the atomic and nuclear sectors.

    Jobs and Opportunities

    Nuclear fusion has boosted the British economy by £1.4 billion, creating about 4,000 jobs a year [4]. Government investment in fusion has also produced a more skilled workforce, creating knowledge transfer between industries, benefitting robotics, artificial intelligence and the new material development. UKAEA CEO, Professor Ian Chapman said “Our mission is to lead the delivery of sustainable fusion energy and maximise scientific and economic benefit.” [4]

    The new UKAEA facility in Rotherham will bring with it 40 new jobs [5]. Chief executive officer of the Nuclear AMRC, Andrew Stoner, has said: “This development has the potential to create many jobs in the local supply chain as fusion technology matures. This is a huge deal for Sheffield and the North, and we are really pleased to have played a part in this and to be working with UKAEA” [3]. Ontop of job opportunities created by the facility itself, requirements for specialist materials at regular frequency demands the work of the local manufacturers, providing further employment opportunities for local companies. Mayor of Sheffield, Dan Jarvis, has said that this will cause “boosting the region’s economy and highlighting our world-leading specialisms in advanced manufacturing” [3]. The £2 million investment from the local Sheffield Growth Fund will help further improve the status of the Advanced Manufacturing Park as an area of high-value employment within the UK.

    Further Information on Other Projects

    Culham centre for Fusion Energy (CCFE) is one of the world's leading fusion research laboratories. It is run by the UKAEA and host to JET which has set world records for plasma performance since operations began in 1983. It is operated in conjunction with the European Commission (managed by the EUROfusion consortium.) MAST (Mega Amp spherical tokamak) also operates out of Culham, researching upgrades to tokamak reactors in the form of a new style of divertor. This should be capable of managing the power load of a tokamak instead of needing periodic replacement after being worn down inducing large operation costs.

    Furthermore, the UKAEA run several facilities and programs out of Culham and surrounding areas to train young and new people in the field such as OAS (Oxfordshire advanced skills), a training centre for Oxfordshire businesses to offer engineering apprenticeships. They also run a Materials Research Facility aimed at both University and Industry users and RACE (remote applications in challenging environments facility) for Research and Development in the robotics field.

    STEP

    The main project being worked on currently by UKAEA is the Spherical Tokamak for energy production (STEP) facility. This is a UK based fusion power station currently being planned and conceptualized. UKAEA aims to have the reactor producing a net gain of energy by 2040[6] and are operating on an inital budget of £220 million[6] to design the reactor. It is currently unclear how much of his budget will be spent on construction and what the exact final cost of the project will be.
