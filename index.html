    Intro to fusion physics

    Nuclear fusion reactions occur to combine light atomic nuclei, releasing energy in the process. To overcome the natural electrostatic repulsion (Coulomb Force) between the reaction nuclei, they have to reach extremely high temperatures (millions of degrees) to form a plasma. There are multiple methods for creating and forming this plasma currently being tested. The main fuel currently being tested for reactors consists of Deuterium and Tritium.

    The fusion between deuterium and tritium (DT) nuclei produces one helium nucleus, one neutron, and great amounts of energy. The images [3] (below) illustrate DT (deuterium-tritium) fusion reactions, in which two very excited, very hot atoms with high energies collide, fusing into one atom and releasing leftover subatomic particles and energy. The third atom produced from the DT reaction is extremely unstable and so it releases the extra neutrons (and leftover energy) in order to become stable again.

    What is the UKAEA?

    The UK Atomic Energy Authority researches nuclear fusion and sustainable technologies with the goal of creating a sustainable future for the UK, benefiting the economy and the nations standing as a leader in nuclear energy.[3]

    The new UKAEA facility will create many jobs for the local area in Sheffield, and the wider supply chain across the UK. It will be based in the Advanced Manufacturing Park (AMP) in Rotherham alongside the Advanced Manufacturing Research Centre (AMRC).

    The Advanced Manufacturing Research Centre is a world-leading research centre based in Rotherham, with over 120 partners including Boeing, Rolls-Royce and Airbus.
