    Introduction to fusion physics

    Nuclear fusion reactions occur to combine light atomic nuclei, releasing energy in the process. To overcome the natural electrostatic repulsion (Coulomb Force) between the reaction nuclei, they have to reach extremely high temperatures (millions of degrees) to form a plasma. There are multiple methods for creating and forming this plasma currently being tested. The main fuel currently being tested for reactors consists of Deuterium and Tritium.

    The fusion between deuterium and tritium (DT) nuclei produces one helium nucleus, one neutron, and great amounts of energy. The image [1] (below) illustrates DT (deuterium-tritium) fusion reactions, in which two very excited, very hot atoms with high energies collide, fusing into one atom and releasing leftover subatomic particles and energy. The third atom produced from the DT reaction is extremely unstable and so it releases the extra neutrons (and leftover energy) in order to become stable again.

    Current issues with fusion

    Nuclear fusion processes can compromise the structural integrity of the materials encompassing the reactor vessel, leading to costly and dangerous consequences if not addressed. This not only is a problem in contemporary fusion facilities but also for plans of commercial reactors as high maintenance costs and downtime replacing vital components of the reactor can discourage further investment in this sector.

    These processes include Thermally-induced defects, Oxidation, Neutron-induced degradation\embrittlement and transmutation. Also, the risk of producing radioactive waste from these various high energy neutron interactions poses another problem with the production of clean and sustainable energy.

    As it stands, no such material exists to date which is capable of withstanding all these effects under such strenuous conditions over a sustained period. This means that more research into this needs to be done.

    What is the UKAEA?

    The UK Atomic Energy Authority researches nuclear fusion and sustainable technologies with the goal of creating a sustainable future for the UK, benefiting the economy and the nations standing as a leader in nuclear energy.[2]

    The new UKAEA facility will create many jobs for the local area in Sheffield, and the wider supply chain across the UK. It will be based in the Advanced Manufacturing Park (AMP) in Rotherham alongside the Advanced Manufacturing Research Centre (AMRC).

    The Advanced Manufacturing Research Centre is a world-leading research centre based in Rotherham, with over 120 partners including Boeing, Rolls-Royce and Airbus. Run by the University of Sheffield, the AMRC will be home to the new £22m UKAEA facility and will focus research into finding suitable materials for fusion, joining techniques, and additive manufacturing. [3] At this new facility, the UKAEA aims to achieve commercial nuclear fusion by utilising cost effective and technical manufacturing to overcome the major issues preventing it. This may be done by using the above techniques and modularisation to efficiently manage sections of the whole design program.

    How can the University get involved?

    Primarily, research at the new UKAEA facility will consist of materials research and manufacturing research. Researchers may collaborate with the University of Sheffield’s department of material science and engineering where the aforementioned techniques are researched. The Physics department research largely does not overlap with this sector. This means that to effectively assist in the research carried out at the facility large changes would need to be made in the department which cannot be justified by the size of the current investment. In conclusion, it would be more advisable for the materials and engineering departments to assist in the research as it more directly connects with current studies in these fields.
